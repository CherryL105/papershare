Abstract

Heart failure remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality, yet no approved therapies effectively prevent or reverse pathological cardiac fibrosis and the associated decline in cardiac function1,2,3,4. Chronic inflammation is a central driver of pathological fibrosis after ischaemic or haemodynamic stress, but strategies that locally rebalance injurious and reparative immune responses without systemic immunosuppression are lacking5,6. Dendritic cells (DCs) are key regulators of immune activation and tolerance, providing an opportunity for therapeutic immune reprogramming in cardiac diseases7,8. Here we show that engineered immunosuppressive and fibrosis-targeted DCs (iCDCs) effectively protect against pathological cardiac remodelling. In mouse models of ischaemia–reperfusion injury, myocardial infarction and pressure overload, iCDC therapy reduced inflammatory cardiac fibrosis, improved cardiac perfusion and preserved contractility. Mechanistically, iCDCs conferred sustained cardioprotection directly by suppressing immune and stromal cell activation or indirectly through promoting clonal expansion of regulatory T cells. Importantly, in a non-human primate model of myocardial infarction, iCDC therapy also reduced cardiac fibrosis, improved cardiac perfusion and contractile function without inducing systemic toxicity. These findings establish lesion-targeted immune modulation as a feasible strategy to control cardiac fibrosis and identify engineered dendritic cells as a promising therapeutic platform for treating cardiac remodelling and heart failure.

Main

Heart failure is a major global health burden, with an estimated prevalence exceeding 56 million individuals worldwide1,2,3. Its prevalence is anticipated to increase by up to 46% by 2030, driven by an ageing population, increased risk factors, the low effectiveness of new therapies and improved survival9. Despite advances in pharmacological and device-based interventions, the 5-year survival rate after a diagnosis of heart failure remains less than 50%10,11. The prognosis is particularly poor in patients with advanced heart failure, who are frequently refractory or intolerant to standard therapies owing to haemodynamic instability and multiorgan dysfunction4. These challenges underscore the urgent need for new and mechanism-oriented therapeutic approaches to complement existing strategies.

Cardiac remodelling is a fundamental process and a determinant of the clinical course in the pathophysiology of heart failure12,13. After myocardial injury, a complex interplay among immune cells, cardiomyocytes, fibroblasts and endothelial cells triggers an integrated innate and adaptive immune response. In the acute phase, the inflammatory response induced by the innate system upregulates a portfolio of cytoprotective factors that provide the heart with a short-term adaptation to increased stress5,14. However, when this response becomes dysregulated or prolonged, it promotes maladaptive remodelling—characterized by persistent inflammation, interstitial fibrosis and loss of contractile function—which contributes to irreversible myocardial damage and progressive heart failure5,6,15. Modulating this inflammatory milieu has therefore emerged as a promising therapeutic avenue. Recent clinical strategies to suppress maladaptive inflammation in cardiovascular disease have included the use of systemic agents, such as colchicine, and cytokine-targeting biologics, such as canakinumab (a monoclonal antibody against IL-1β)6,16. Although these approaches have shown modest efficacy, their clinical use is constrained by systemic immunosuppression, transient therapeutic effects and off-target effects that may impair beneficial immune functions that are essential for host defence and tissue repair6,16. Moreover, strategies targeting single-molecule targets have limited efficacy in combating inflammation, given its complex and multifactorial nature. These limitations underscore a pressing need for multipronged and durable immunomodulatory strategies that can selectively target pathogenic immune circuits within specific organs or lesion sites.

Recent advances in dendritic cell (DC) therapy have highlighted its potential as an immunomodulatory strategy in heart failure. DCs, as professional antigen-presenting cells, are positioned at the nexus of innate and adaptive immunity and are capable of orchestrating context-specific immune responses17. While DCs are traditionally known for their ability to activate T cells, tolerogenic DCs confer immunosuppressive effects through different mechanisms involving regulatory cytokine production, expression of co-inhibitory ligands, antigen-specific suppression of effector T cells and regulatory T (Treg) cell induction8. These features render DCs an attractive but underexplored platform for resolving inflammation and promoting tissue repair in diseases such as heart failure.

Despite these promising characteristics, several challenges remain in translating DC-based therapies for cardiac injury, including the phenotypic instability of immature or tolerogenic DCs in inflammatory environments and inefficient trafficking to damaged myocardium7,18. To overcome these barriers, we developed a platform of engineered DCs expressing an immunosuppressive chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-like construct, termed iCDCs. Considering the critical roles of fibroblast activation protein (FAP)-positive fibroblasts in cardiac fibrosis after injury, FAP-targeted CAR-T cells and CAR macrophages have been developed to attenuate fibrosis and improve cardiac function in mice by eliminating these pathogenic fibroblasts19,20,21,22. Building on this targeting strategy, and informed by our previous finding that dual expression of CTLA4-Ig and PD-L1 confers stable immune tolerance capacity in human embryonic stem cell-derived DC-like cells23,24, we equipped DCs with a FAP-specific single-chain variable fragment (scFv; hereafter, FAP-scFv) for targeted delivery, along with constitutive expression of CTLA4-Ig, PD-L1 and IL-10 to enhance immunomodulatory capacity. This design enables more localized suppression of pathological immune responses and fibrosis within the myocardium while preserving systemic immune competence.

In this study, we demonstrate that iCDCs effectively mitigate inflammation and fibrosis, and enhance cardiac function in both ischaemic and pressure-overload-induced heart failure models. Mechanistically, iCDCs confer cardioprotection through pleiotropic and sustained mechanisms involving both direct suppression of innate and stromal cell activation and the durable clonal expansion of regulatory T cells. Finally, using a non-human primate (NHP) model of myocardial infarction (MI), we show that iCDCs exert durable cardioprotective effects without systemic toxicity, supporting their translational potential as a targeted immunotherapy for heart failure.

Phenotypic profiling of FAP-targeted iCDCs

To enable spatially targeted immunosuppression within injured myocardium, we engineered bone-marrow-derived DCs to express a FAP-specific scFv, co-delivering three key immunomodulatory factors: CTLA4-Ig, PD-L1 and IL-10 (Fig. 1a and Extended Data Fig. 1a). CTLA4 and PD-1 immune checkpoint pathways are central to peripheral tolerance, in which they operate at different stages of an immune response25,26. CTLA4 is considered to operate at the initial stage to inhibit naive T cell activation, whereas the PD-1 pathway regulates previously activated T cells at the later stage27. IL-10, a major anti-inflammatory cytokine, has garnered considerable interest for therapeutic use owing to its broad immunosuppressive and tissue-reparative properties28,29. The lentiviral construct encodes an anti-FAP-scFv fused to the mouse CD8α hinge and transmembrane domains, with payloads linked through internal ribosome entry sites (IRES). Flow cytometry analysis confirmed efficient lentiviral transduction, with approximately one-third of CD11c+ DCs expressing surface scFv (Extended Data Fig. 1b).

These FAP-targeted immune suppressive DCs—termed iCDCs—adopted a distinct tolerogenic phenotype. Compared with the vector-only controls (vecDCs), iCDCs secreted substantially higher levels of IL-10 and CTLA4-Ig (Extended Data Fig. 1c) and, in response to strong stimulation with lipopolysaccharide (LPS) + TNF treatment, exhibited reduced surface expression of the co-stimulatory molecules CD80, CD86 and MHC-II (Fig. 1b and Extended Data Fig. 1d). In parallel, they displayed elevated expression of the inhibitory checkpoint molecule TIM3 after stimulation (Fig. 1b and Extended Data Fig. 1d). Transcriptomic profiling revealed marked downregulation of chemotaxis, immune activation and pro-inflammatory signalling pathways (for example, NF-κB, MAPK and IL-17) and upregulation of anti-inflammatory and pro-Treg-cell differentiation pathways (Fig. 1c and Extended Data Fig. 1e,f).

Functionally, iCDCs robustly suppressed T cell proliferation and activation. Transcriptomic analysis of T cells co-cultured with iCDCs or vecDCs confirmed iCDC-mediated suppression of genes involved in the activation of immune responses, chemotaxis and inflammatory response (Fig. 1d and Extended Data Fig. 1g,h). In carboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester (CFSE)-based co-culture assays with T cells stimulated with CD3 and CD28, iCDCs inhibited T cell proliferation compared with the vecDC controls (Fig. 1e). T cells exposed to iCDCs also displayed a phenotypic shift marked by diminished frequencies of CD69+ T cells, T helper type 17 (TH17) cells, central memory (TCM) and effector memory T (TEM) cells, and increased proportions of Treg cells and naive T cells (Fig. 1f).

To elucidate the mechanisms of iCDC-mediated suppression, we performed blocking experiments with neutralizing antibodies against IL-10, CTLA4 and PD-L1 (Fig. 1g). Inhibition of any single factor partially reversed iCDC-induced inhibition of T cell proliferation, and inhibition of PD-L1 or CTLA4 reversed iCDC-induced suppression of T cell activation (Fig. 1h–j). Treg cell induction was markedly impaired by blockade of any of the three suppressive axes (Fig. 1i,j), suggesting their non-redundant contribution to this iCDC function. By contrast, inhibition of any single factor had no obvious effect on TH17 polarization (Fig. 1i). Moreover, the frequencies of naive, TCM cell and TEM cell populations remained largely unaffected across blockade conditions (Extended Data Fig. 1i).

Together, these results establish that iCDCs are stably tolerogenic DCs that suppress T cell activation and induce Treg cells, underscoring their potential as multifunctional immunomodulators for targeted immune reprogramming in inflammatory diseases.

FAP-targeted iCDCs reduce ischaemic fibrosis

To establish the rationale for targeting FAP in ischaemic heart disease, we characterized the spatiotemporal dynamics of FAP expression after ischaemic injury. Spatial transcriptomic profiling demonstrated progressive induction of Fap within infarct and border zones from day 3 through day 14 after MI (Fig. 2a). Consistently, immunoblotting revealed increased myocardial FAP protein after infarction with the same kinetics (Extended Data Fig. 2a). Quantitative PCR (qPCR) analysis further showed robust induction of Fap in the heart after MI, but not in the liver, kidney, lung or spleen across the same time window (Extended Data Fig. 2b). Similar to the myocardial FAP expression after MI, myocardial FAP expression was also increased from day 3 to day 14 after ischaemia–reperfusion (I/R) injury (Extended Data Fig. 2c). Immunofluorescence staining demonstrated co-localization of FAP with vimentin+ fibroblasts in the peri-infarct region, becoming more prominent between day 3 and day 7 (Extended Data Fig. 2d). Together, these data indicate a temporal window of elevated cardiac FAP expression after injury that informed the timing of intervention.

To define the optimal timing of iCDC administration, iCDCs were administered on day 1, 3 or 7 after I/R injury. Among these schedules, treatment on day 3 yielded the greatest therapeutic benefit, reflected by improved left ventricular ejection fraction (EF%) and fractional shortening (FS%), and reduced left ventricular internal dimensions in systole (LVIDs) at day 28, along with no marked changes in the left ventricular internal dimensions in diastole (LVIDd; Extended Data Fig. 2e,f). Histological assessment using Sirius Red staining revealed that day 3 treatment, but not day 1 or day 7 treatment, substantially reduced cardiac fibrosis (Extended Data Fig. 2g). Dose–response analysis revealed comparable improvements in EF and FS across both low (1.5 × 106) and high (3.0 × 106) doses, without notable changes in LVIDs or LVIDd (Extended Data Fig. 2h,i). Sirus Red staining confirmed that both doses attenuated I/R-induced cardiac fibrosis (Extended Data Fig. 2j). These findings support the use of 1.5 × 106 iCDCs administered on day 3 after injury as the reasonable regimen for subsequent studies.

Using DiR-based ex vivo imaging, we observed preferential enrichment of iCDCs in the infarcted heart, peaking at day 3 and persisting to day 7, whereas vecDCs, DCs expressing CTLA4-Ig, PD-L1 and IL-10 without FAP-scFv (CPIDCs), and DCs expressing GFP-specific scFv, CTLA4-Ig, PD-L1 and IL-10 (GFP-CPIDCs) showed minimal myocardial signal (Fig. 2b and Extended Data Fig. 2k,l). All of the groups exhibited the expected signal in lung, spleen and liver, consistent with DC trafficking (Extended Data Fig. 2m–o). Functionally, iCDC treatment markedly improved the EF% and FS% at 28 days after MI (Fig. 2c,d and Extended Data Fig. 2p,q). Moreover, iCDC therapy preserved left ventricular geometry, as indicated by a reduction in LVIDs and LVIDd (Extended Data Fig. 2q,r). Sirius Red staining revealed reduced fibrosis in both infarct and remote zones of the myocardium in iCDC-treated mice (Fig. 2e,f), supported by decreased expression of fibrosis-associated proteins (Fig. 2g). Moreover, iCDC therapy promoted cardiomyocyte preservation in the infarct region and enhanced neovascularization in both infarct and peri-infarct areas, as evidenced by increased numbers of viable cardiomyocytes in infarct zones (Fig. 2h) and elevated CD31+ vessel density in the infarct and border zone (Fig. 2i). The therapeutic benefits of iCDCs—including improved cardiac function, reduced cardiac fibrosis, enhanced cardiomyocyte preservation in the infarct region, and increased neovascularization in the infarct and peri-infarct regions—were abolished in the absence of the FAP-scFv component (Fig. 2d–i and Extended Data Fig. 2q,r), indicating that FAP-scFv-mediated targeting is indispensable for the in vivo therapeutic efficacy of iCDCs.

We extended these observations to a perfused ischaemia model (Fig. 2j). iCDC treatment preserved cardiac contractility and geometry over a 12-week period compared with the vecDC controls, as shown by elevated EF% and FS%, and reduced LVIDs, along with no marked alteration in LVIDd (Fig. 2k and Extended Data Fig. 2t,u). Histological analysis with Sirius Red staining demonstrated a markedly reduced fibrotic area in iCDC-treated hearts (Fig. 2l), accompanied by decreased expression of fibronectin, periostin and α-SMA (Fig. 2m). Moreover, iCDC therapy enhanced neovascularization in ischaemic areas, as evidenced by elevated CD31+ capillary density (Extended Data Fig. 2v). Differentially expressed gene analysis in iCDC versus vecDC by RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) also revealed the upregulation of genes promoting angiogenesis and endothelial cell morphogenesis (Extended Data Fig. 2w).

To define the minimal effective immunomodulatory program, we tested DCs with dual-factor expression (CTLA4-Ig + PD-L1 (FAP-CP), CTLA4-Ig + IL-10 (FAP-CI) and PD-L1 + IL-10 (FAP-PI)) or single-factor expression (IL-10 (FAP-I), CTLA4-Ig (FAP-C) and PD-L1 (FAP-P)), all retaining the FAP-targeting scFv domain. In contrast to iCDCs, none of the DCs expressing FAP dual factor or FAP single factor retained the therapeutic activities for cardiac functional improvement or antifibrotic effects as indicated by echocardiography, fibrosis histology and protein analysis (Extended Data Fig. 3a–j). These results demonstrate that the full complement of FAP-scFv, IL-10, CTLA4-Ig and PD-L1 is required for in vivo efficacy of iCDCs.

Collectively, these results demonstrate that FAP-targeted iCDCs preferentially home to injured myocardium, mitigate fibrotic remodelling, enhance angiogenesis and preserve cardiac function in both I/R and MI models. Mechanistic dissection further shows that therapeutic efficacy requires both FAP targeting and the complete immunomodulatory payload (IL-10, CTLA4-Ig and PD-L1), establishing iCDCs as a localized immunoregulatory strategy for treating ischaemic heart disease.

iCDCs ameliorate pressure-overload-induced heart failure

To assess the therapeutic potential of iCDCs in non-ischaemic heart failure, we used the transverse aortic constriction (TAC) model (Fig. 1a). Ex vivo fluorescence imaging revealed preferential enrichment of DiR-labelled iCDCs within the heart after TAC during days 3 to 7 after infusion compared with GFP-CPIDCs (Extended Data Fig. 4a). Temporal profiling revealed progressive upregulation of cardiac FAP protein after TAC, with peak expression observed between 4 and 6 weeks after surgery (Extended Data Fig. 5a). On the basis of this expression window, we administered DiR-labelled iCDCs intravenously at 4 weeks and 6 weeks after TAC (Extended Data Fig. 4b). Parallel organ imaging demonstrated the expected distribution of DCs to liver, spleen, lung and kidney across groups based on the normal trafficking of DCs (Extended Data Fig. 5b–d).

To evaluate the therapeutic efficacy, cardiac function was serially assessed by echocardiography (Extended Data Fig. 4b). iCDC-treated mice displayed markedly improved systolic function compared with the vecDC controls, as indicated by increased EF% and FS% at 8 weeks after TAC, with no notable changes in LVIDs and LVIDd (Extended Data Figs. 4c and 5e,f). Histologically, Sirius Red staining demonstrated markedly decreased fibrosis in iCDC-treated hearts (Extended Data Fig. 4d), which was corroborated by reduced protein expression of fibronectin, periostin and α-SMA (Extended Data Fig. 4e). Consistent with observations in iCDC-treated ischaemic models, immunofluorescence staining showed a trend toward increased CD31+ vessel density, indicating enhanced myocardial angiogenesis (Extended Data Fig. 5g). Importantly, Kaplan–Meier analysis showed that iCDC treatment substantially prolonged the survival of TAC mice compared with the vecDC-treated groups (Extended Data Fig. 4f).

We next evaluated the efficacy of iCDCs in TAC mice with advanced heart failure (EF ≦ 40%; Extended Data Fig. 4g). Dual-dose iCDC administration in this advanced-stage setting markedly improved cardiac function within 4 weeks, with sustained recovery of EF% and FS%, without notable changes in LVIDs and LVIDd (Extended Data Figs. 4h and 5h–i). Fibrosis quantification by Sirius Red staining and western blot analysis confirmed substantial reductions in myocardial collagen deposition and fibrotic protein expression (Extended Data Fig. 4i,j). Furthermore, iCDC therapy enhanced angiogenesis, as demonstrated by increased CD31+ vessel density in the myocardium (Extended Data Fig. 4k). Notably, survival analysis showed a notable improvement in overall survival among iCDC-treated TAC mice compared with the vecDC-treated groups (Extended Data Fig. 4l).

Collectively, these results establish that iCDCs effectively ameliorate cardiac dysfunction and fibrosis in a non-ischaemic model of pressure overload, promote neovascularization and prolong survival, even in advanced disease settings. These findings highlight the translational potential of iCDCs for treating diverse forms of heart failure beyond ischaemic injury.

Safety of iCDCs in the mouse models

We evaluated the systemic safety of iCDC therapy across the MI, I/R and TAC mouse models. Continuous ECG recording for 1 week in MI mice showed no increase in arrhythmic burden after iCDC treatment, including non-sustained or sustained ventricular tachycardia events or premature ventricular contractions, relative to the MI controls (Extended Data Fig. 6a). Histopathological examination of major organs revealed no evidence of treatment-related toxicity. Haematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining of lung, kidney and liver showed normal tissue architecture in iCDC-treated animals of the I/R injury (Extended Data Fig. 6b,d) and TAC (Extended Data Fig. 6f) models. Consistently, serum markers of renal and hepatic function—alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), blood urea nitrogen (BUN) and creatinine (Cr)—were comparable between the sham group and the groups of the MI, I/R and TAC models (Extended Data Fig. 6c,e,g). Further analysis of circulating cytokine profiles revealed that iCDCs selectively reduced pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as IL-6 and TNF, without notably altering levels of IFNγ, IL-17α, IL-4 and IL-10 (Extended Data Fig. 6h), suggesting a relatively targeted anti-inflammatory effect mediated by iCDCs and reinforcing their therapeutic potential for modulating inflammation in heart failure models.

Together, these data indicate that iCDC therapy is well tolerated in mouse models of ischaemic and pressure-overload heart diseases, with no detectable arrhythmogenic risk, systemic toxicity or adverse effects on renal or hepatic function.

iCDCs suppress T cell activation

To identify the mechanisms by which iCDCs ameliorate cardiac remodelling, we performed integrated bulk and single-cell transcriptomic analyses in the I/R model (Fig. 1a). Bulk RNA-seq analysis of cardiac tissue at day 7 after injury revealed that iCDC treatment downregulated pro-inflammatory gene programs related to leukocyte chemotaxis, cytokine–cytokine receptor interaction and immune activation, while upregulating pathways linked to contractile function, cytoskeletal organization and calcium handling (Fig. 3a and Extended Data Fig. 7a,b).

Single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq) analysis of 112,794 cardiac interstitial cells identified 10 major cell types, including T cells, macrophages, DCs, neutrophils, B cells, natural killer (NK) cells, mast cells, fibroblasts and endothelial cells (Fig. 3b and Extended Data Fig. 8a). iCDC treatment resulted in marked shifts in immune cell composition, with notable reductions in neutrophils, B cells and T cells, as confirmed by both scRNA-seq and flow cytometry analysis of cardiac tissues at day 7 after I/R (Fig. 3c,d).

Focusing on T cells, uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) of 14,869 CD3+ cells revealed 11 transcriptionally distinct clusters. iCDC-treated hearts exhibited a redistribution of T cell phenotypes, including a reduction in pro-inflammatory subsets (for example, γδ T cells and TEM cells), and an enrichment of naive and regulatory T cells (Fig. 3e,f and Extended Data Fig. 8b). Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in T cells highlighted suppression of pathways involved in T cell activation and proliferation, while promoting immune-regulatory gene expression (Extended Data Fig. 8c). Heat-map analysis further demonstrated downregulation of effector molecules associated with cell migration, pro-inflammation, and differentiation of TH1, TH2 and TH17 cells to cytotoxic T cells (Fig. 3g).

Flow cytometry analysis confirmed these findings, revealing reduced frequencies of TH17 cells, TCM (CD44highCD62Lhigh) cells and TEM (CD44highCD62Llow) cells in iCDC-treated hearts compared with the vecDC controls (Fig. 3h and Extended Data Fig. 8d). Concurrently, an expansion of Treg cells (CD25+FOXP3+) and naive T cells (CD44−CD62L+) after iCDC treatment was observed, along with a trend toward reduced T cell activation, as indicated by a lower proportion of CD69+ cells (Fig. 3h and Extended Data Fig. 8d). Longitudinal flow cytometry profiling showed increased percentages of Treg cells in the myocardium both 7 days and 28 days after MI with a switch to the memory-like Treg cells (CD44highCD62Llow) at the latter date, accompanied by the normalization of overall T cell activation (CD69+), suggesting a durable immunosuppressive environment after the resolution of the initial MI-induced immune responses (Fig. 3i and Extended Data Fig. 8e). To investigate the origin and clonality of iCDC-induced Treg cells, cardiac and circulating CD4+ T cells were isolated from MI-injured mice with or without iCDC treatment to perform single-cell TCR sequencing. Consistent with our flow cytometry results, single-cell TCR-sequencing analysis also revealed the elevated proportion of Treg cells in iCDC-treated mouse hearts and peripheral blood (Extended Data Fig. 8f–h). This analysis further revealed preferential clonal expansion of Treg cells in the heart after iCDC treatment, with limited overlap between cardiac and blood TCR repertoires, consistent with localized antigen-driven Treg cell selection and persistence (Fig. 3j). Differentially expressed gene analysis in Treg cells between MI and iCDC-treated hearts showed that iCDC treatment significantly elevated the expression of key genes associated with functional activation (Ccr8, Maf, Tnfrsf4, Tnfrsf9, Tnfrsf18, Tgfβ1) and memory/survival maintenance (Ets1, Gimap3, Gimap5, Gpx1, Gpx4, Il2rg, Lgals1, Il2rb, Nfkb1, Cox5a, Txn1, Prdx2 and Prdx1), accompanied by enrichment of pathways related to T cell activation and proliferation, indicating enhanced activation and persistence of these cells in the inflamed microenvironment (Fig. 3k and Extended Data Fig. 8i). In the expanded Treg cells, representing clonally proliferated Treg cell populations, we observed increased expression of genes linked to antigen-specific expansion (Sipa1, Trbc2 and Trbv29), functional activation (Gzmb, Lime1, Selenok and Il31ra) and long-term survival (Aven, Cct6a, Ppm1h and Wrn), further suggesting that iCDCs promote sustained, antigen-driven Treg cell expansion in the injured heart (Fig. 3k and Extended Data Fig. 8j). Together, these data demonstrate that iCDC therapy not only enhances Treg cell activation and longevity but also fosters the antigen-specific expansion of regulatory populations critical for immune homeostasis and tissue repair after MI.

Collectively, these data demonstrate that iCDCs drive robust reshaping of cardiac T cell compartments after ischaemic injury, favouring an immunoregulatory phenotype of T cells characterized by the suppression of effector programs and clonal expansion of tissue-resident Treg cells.

iCDCs reprogram immune–stromal networks

To investigate how iCDCs reshape the broader immune and stromal microenvironment, we performed scRNA-seq analysis and flow cytometry profiling of non-T-cell populations in hearts from sham-, I/R-, vecDC- and iCDC-treated I/R mice. scRNA-seq of 67,868 myeloid-derived cells revealed substantial reprogramming of the macrophage compartment after iCDC treatment, with a marked shift from CCR2+ pro-inflammatory subsets (for example, Ccr2+MHCIIhigh, Spp1+ and IFN-response macrophages) toward Ccr2−MHCII+ tissue-resident macrophages (Fig. 4a,b and Extended Data Fig. 9a). This was accompanied by the downregulation of pathways involved in phagocytosis, IL-6 production, and antigen processing and presentation (Extended Data Fig. 9b). These phenotypic transitions were confirmed by flow cytometry, which demonstrated a decrease in CCR2+ macrophages and a reciprocal enrichment of reparative TIM4+ and CCR2−MHC-II+ macrophages (Fig. 4c and Extended Data Fig. 9c). Analysis of DC subsets within the myeloid-derived cells confirmed their immune regulatory status with the downregulation of immune regulation, antigen presentation and pro-inflammatory signalling pathways, as well as the upregulation of anti-inflammatory signalling pathways (Extended Data Fig. 9d,e).

Similarly, scRNA-seq analysis of 8,428 neutrophils identified 5 subsets, including homeostatic subtypes (Mmp8+, Cstdc4+ and Csf3rhigh) and proinflammatory subtypes (Cd14high and Icam1+; Fig. 4d and Extended Data Fig. 10a). Further analysis revealed suppression of Cd14high and Icam1+ subsets in the iCDC group (Fig. 4e), accompanied by transcriptomic reduction in inflammatory phenotypes with the downregulation of chemotaxis and pro-inflammatory pathways (Extended Data Fig. 10b). These findings were validated by decreased CD14+ and ICAM-1+ neutrophil subsets in flow cytometry analyses of iCDC-treated heart (Fig. 4f and Extended Data Fig. 10c).

In the B cell compartment, scRNA-seq analysis of 11,164 cells identified a decrease in activated and plasma cell subtypes after iCDC therapy, accompanied by the downregulation of pathways involved in immunoglobulin production, B cell differentiation and activation (Fig. 4g,h and Extended Data Fig. 10d,e). Flow cytometry analysis confirmed reduced infiltration and activation of cardiac B cells, including diminished subsets of CD69+, CD80+ and IgG+ B cells (Fig. 4i and Extended Data Fig. 10f).

scRNA-seq analysis of 72,895 cardiac fibroblasts identified distinct subpopulations on the basis of differential gene expression profiles: quiescent/haemostatic subtypes (F-SL, F-SH) and activated subtypes (F-Myo, F-Act, F-IFNs) (Fig. 4j and Extended Data Fig. 11a,b). Further analysis revealed a reduced abundance of the fibrogenic F-Myo subset and an improved proportion of F-SL in iCDC-treated hearts (Fig. 4k), along with decreased expression of genes related to collagen fibril organization, cell-substrate adhesion and would healing (Extended Data Fig. 11c,d). An extracellular matrix (ECM) regulator score, reflecting profibrotic gene activity, was notably lower in the iCDC group (Fig. 4l and Extended Data Fig. 11e,f). Flow cytometry analysis corroborated these findings, revealing reduced numbers of total fibroblasts and fewer MHC-II+ fibroblasts (Fig. 4m and Extended Data Fig. 11g).

To determine the dependency of iCDC effects on T cells, we conducted TCRαβ+ T cell depletion before MI induction. TCRαβ+ T cell depletion had no marked impact on the iCDC-mediated increase in resident macrophages and decrease in activated neutrophils, B cells or fibroblasts in the post-MI heart (Fig. 4n), demonstrating a dispensable role for αβ+ T cells in mediating the effects of iCDCs on resident macrophages, neutrophils, B cells and fibroblasts. Finally, co-culture experiments using iCDCs with sorted macrophages, B cells, neutrophils and fibroblasts confirmed that iCDCs directly suppress the activation of each target population (Fig. 4s), as evidenced by the decreased percentage of MHC-II+ in macrophages (Fig. 4o), IgG+ in activated B cells (Fig. 4q), CD14+ and ICAM-1+ neutrophils (Fig. 4p) and MHC-II+ activated fibroblasts in the co-culture, accompanied by decreased mRNA levels of Fn1 (encoding fibronectin), Postn (encoding periostin) and Acta2 in activated fibroblasts in the co-culture (Fig. 4r and Extended Data Fig. 11h).

Together, these findings demonstrate that iCDCs orchestrate both T-cell-dependent and direct immunomodulatory effects to reprogram macrophages, neutrophils, B cells and fibroblasts toward reparative phenotypes in injured hearts. This coordinated cellular reprogramming establishes a tolerogenic and antifibrotic niche that supports effective myocardial repair after ischaemic injury.

iCDCs protect primate hearts after MI

To assess the translational potential of iCDC therapy, we established a NHP model of MI through permanent ligation of the left anterior descending coronary artery. Autologous DCs were engineered to express a FAP-specific scFv along with immunomodulatory payloads (IL-10, PD-L1 and CTLA4-Ig). Flow cytometry analysis confirmed robust scFv expression in CD11c+ DCs, accompanied by elevated secretion of IL-10 and CTLA4-Ig and reduced expression of the co-stimulatory molecules MHC-II, CD80 and CD86—features indicative of a tolerogenic phenotype (Fig. 5a–c and Extended Data Fig. 12a).

After intravenous administration of iCDCs on day 3 after MI, cardiac function was monitored longitudinally through echocardiography and cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) (Fig. 5d). Echocardiography analysis revealed that iCDC treatment induced sustained improvements in EF% (51.79 ± 1.208 in iCDC versus 42.76 ± 1.336 in MI) at 3 months after MI compared with the untreated MI controls (Fig. 5e). ΔEF% analysis further confirmed this functional improvement (8.229 ± 1.915 in iCDC versus −3.705 ± 1.812 in MI; Fig. 5e). Cardiac MRI assessments corroborated these findings, showing a marked improvement in EF% (48.31 ± 1.720 in iCDC versus 42.19 ± 1.684 in MI) at 3 months after MI, without notable changes in end-systolic volume (ESV) and end-diastolic volume (EDV) compared with the untreated MI controls (Fig. 5f and Extended Data Fig. 12b). ΔEF% analysis by MRI also indicated superior cardiac recovery in the iCDC group (8.976 ± 1.258 in iCDC versus 1.656 ± 1.816 in MI; Fig. 5f). Cardiac MRI with late gadolinium enhancement (LGE) revealed a trend toward reduction in infarct size in iCDC-treated hearts (Fig. 5g,h), a finding corroborated by Sirius Red staining, which showed reduced fibrosis in both infarcted and remote myocardial zones (Fig. 5i,j). Western blot analysis further confirmed downregulation of fibrotic markers, including fibronectin, periostin and α-SMA, across the infarct, border and remote zones (Fig. 5l).

In addition to mitigating fibrotic remodelling, iCDC therapy preserved viable myocardium, as indicated by increased cTnI+ cardiomyocyte area in the infarct zone (Fig. 5m). Immunofluorescence analysis showed a marked increase in CD31+ vessel density (Fig. 5n), and an increase in α-SMA+ arteriolar structures (Fig. 5o) in the infarct and peri-infarct regions, collectively indicating enhanced angiogenesis.

Comprehensive safety analyses supported the favourable safety profile of iCDC therapy. Haematological indices, including white blood cell counts, platelet counts and haemoglobin, remained stable over a 3-month period after treatment (Extended Data Fig. 13a). Serum biochemical markers of hepatic and renal functions (ALT, AST, Cr, albumin/globulin ratio) were unchanged across timepoints (Extended Data Fig. 13a). Cytokine profiling revealed no marked alteration in IL-4, IL-6, IL-10, IL-17A or IFNγ after treatment, suggesting immune modulation of injured heart without overt systemic immune suppression (Extended Data Fig. 13b). Histological analysis of brain, liver, kidney, lung, spleen, intestine, skin and thyroid tissues showed no pathological abnormalities (Extended Data Fig. 13c), and brain MRI confirmed preserved morphology (Extended Data Fig. 13d). Continuous ECG monitoring for 28 days revealed no increase in ventricular tachycardia or premature ventricular contractions with iCDC therapy (Fig. 5k and Extended Data Fig. 13e).

Collectively, these findings demonstrate that iCDC therapy yields durable structural and functional cardiac improvements in a clinically relevant NHP model of MI, while maintaining systemic safety and immunological balance. These results provide strong preclinical support for advancing iCDCs toward clinical translation in post-infarction cardiac repair.

Discussion

Heart failure remains a progressive, multifactorial syndrome with limited therapeutic options to directly address the interlinked processes of chronic inflammation and pathological remodelling that drive its progression. In this study, building on the dual roles of DCs as regulators of immune tolerance and inflammation, we present a rationally designed immunotherapy platform, iCDCs, which express IL-10, PD-L1 and CTLA4-Ig, and are equipped with a FAP-specific scFv for spatial precision. Importantly, FAP-targeted iCDCs maintained their immunosuppressive phenotype even within the acutely inflamed myocardium—a major barrier for traditional DC therapies. FAP-scFv-mediated recognition of FAP+ fibroblasts enabled preferential homing to fibrotic regions, overcoming the poor trafficking and limited tissue specificity of DCs. Once localized to the heart, iCDCs modulate the local immune landscape by suppressing inflammatory responses, reprogramming myeloid and stromal cell phenotypes, and promoting the expansion of Treg cells, collectively contributing to the attenuation of cardiac remodelling. Beyond T cells, iCDCs can directly influence a broad array of innate immune cells and non-immune cell types, including endothelial cells and fibroblasts, providing a mechanistic basis for the pleiotropic effects observed with iCDC therapy.

Considerable progress has been achieved in FAP-targeted CAR-T-based and CAR-macrophage-based therapy of heart diseases19,20,21,22. The iCDC strategy offers several conceptual and mechanistic advantages over these conventional CAR effector cell approaches. At the cellular level, heart failure progression is fuelled by maladaptive interactions among autoreactive T cells, pro-inflammatory macrophages, activated fibroblasts and neutrophils5,30,31. These interactions perpetuate immune activation, extracellular matrix deposition and irreversible fibrosis. While FAP-directed CAR effector cells target a single pathogenic component through direct cytolytic or phagocytic elimination of activated fibroblasts, iCDCs intervene more broadly in the disease network by reprogramming the inflammatory microenvironment. iCDCs attenuate effector T cell activity, suppress macrophage and neutrophil activation, limit fibroblast-driven matrix production and enhance local Treg-cell-mediated immune tolerance. Moreover, the antifibrotic effect of iCDCs is achieved without direct depletion of fibroblasts, which are important for preserving myocardial structural integrity. By contrast, although FAP-targeted CAR effector cells reduce fibrosis by eliminating FAP+ fibroblasts, this strategy carries potential risks including off-target injury to FAP-expressing cells in non-cardiac tissues, exacerbated local inflammation due to abrupt cell death and the intrinsic inflammatory nature of CAR effector cells, and destabilization of the extracellular matrix, which may compromise mechanical stability during cardiac remodelling. Finally, iCDCs exhibit pro-angiogenic effects that improve the perfusion of injured heart, a benefit that was not reported for the CAR effector cell approach. These distinctions suggest that iCDCs could serve as a safer or non-cytolytic alternative for fibrosis resolution within the landscape of FAP-targeted cell therapies.

Our findings also address longstanding limitations of antigen-specific tolerogenic DC therapies, which often depend on exogenous antigen loading or systemic Treg cell induction8. iCDCs circumvent these challenges by leveraging the endogenous cardiac antigen pool within injured tissue and enabling local immune reprogramming without systemic immune suppression. Furthermore, our study bridges a critical translational gap by demonstrating the safety and efficacy of iCDC therapy in NHPs—a model with greater immunological and physiological relevance to humans than rodents. In the NHP MI model, FAP-iCDCs preserved cardiac function, reduced infarct size, attenuated fibrosis and promoted neovascularization without eliciting systemic toxicity. Comprehensive evaluations of serum biochemistry, cytokine profiles and histopathological analyses across vital organs confirmed the safety profile of iCDC therapy, substantiating its clinical development potential. While the presence and burden of ventricular ectopy was comparable between MI and iCDC-treated animals, suggesting that iCDC therapy does not exacerbate post-infarction arrhythmic risk, larger studies with extended monitoring will be required to define the arrhythmic safety profile of iCDC therapy. Furthermore, more extended pharmacokinetic analyses of the immunomodulatory components expressed by iCDCs and immunosurveillance analyses will be important to further assess any potential long-term risks for clinical translation.

In summary, this work introduces a paradigm for heart failure immunotherapy. By integrating antigen-specific immunosuppression, targeted delivery and long-term immune tolerance, FAP-iCDCs offer a multipronged strategy to reverse cardiac remodelling and restore function. In contrast to current pharmacological or cell-based approaches, iCDCs achieve durable therapeutic effects through pleiotropic immune and stromal reprogramming, with minimal risk of systemic side effects. These findings provide a compelling rationale for advancing iCDCs as a clinically translatable therapy for advanced heart failure.

Methods

Animals and ethics statement

All animal experiments were conducted in accordance with the Guidelines on the Use and Care of Laboratory Animals and were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of the Second Affiliated Hospital, Zhejiang University School of Medicine (2022-249 for mouse experiments; 2024-117 for monkey experiments). C57BL/6J mice (male; aged 6–8 weeks; 20–24 g) were obtained from Beijing Vital River Laboratory Animal Technology. Mice were housed under specific-pathogen-free conditions with free access to food and water, in a controlled environment (temperature: 20–25 °C; humidity: 30–70%; 12 h–12 h light–dark cycle). Cynomolgus monkeys (male; aged 7–10 years; 6–12 kg body weight) were obtained from Huazhen Laboratory Animal Breeding Centre. The monkeys were fed a standard laboratory diet and maintained under a 12 h–12 h light–dark cycle. The monkeys were housed in a controlled environment (temperature: 20–26 °C; humidity: 40–70%; 12 h–12 h light–dark cycle) staffed with trained veterinarians, provided water ad libitum, and fed fresh fruits, vegetables and monkey chow three times daily. No statistical methods were used to predetermine sample size. Sample sizes were chosen on the basis of previous studies using similar experimental designs and taking into account the known variability of the assays. Animals were randomly assigned to experimental groups using random allocation. Investigators were blinded to group allocation during echocardiography assessment and histological analyses.

Cell lines

The HEK293FT cell line (Thermo Fisher Scientific, R70007) was cultured in DMEM (Gibco, C11995500BT) supplemented with 10% FBS (Gibco, 10091-148) and 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin (Gibco, 15140163) at 37 °C with 5% CO2. The cell lines were routinely tested for mycoplasma contamination using PCR-based detection and were confirmed negative.

Construction of plasmids for DC engineering

Mouse and monkey FAP iCDC constructs shared identical expression cassettes and were cloned into a lentiviral vector. The expression cassette contained a FAP-specific single-chain variable fragment (scFv), a CD8α signal peptide, a CD8α hinge and a CD8α transmembrane domain, followed by an expression cassette containing CTLA4-Ig, PD-L1 and IL-10, with IRES elements inserted between genes to ensure co-expression. The source of the gene elements in the construct: anti-mouse FAP-scFv (for mouse scFv, clone 73.3, patent: US 2014/0099340 A1; for monkey, use the human scFv, patent: WO 2021/120526 A1), CD8α signal peptide (for mouse, amino acids 1:27, UniProt: P01731; for monkey, amino acids 1–21, UniProt: P01732), CD8α hinge (for mouse, amino acids 152–196, UniProt: P01731; for monkey, amino acids 151–182, UniProt: P01732); CD8α transmembrane domain (for mouse, amino acids 197–223, UniProt: P01731; for monkey, amino acids 183–206, UniProt: P01732); CTLA4-Ig fusion protein, comprising the CTLA4 signal peptide and extracellular domain (for mouse, amino acids 1–152, UniProt: P09793; for monkey, amino acids 1–161, UniProt: G7PL88) fused to the Fc region of IgG1 (for mouse, amino acids 98–324, UniProt: P01868; for monkey, GenBank: JN984927.1). The construct also included full-length PD-L1 (for mouse, RefSeq: NM_021893.3; for monkey, RefSeq: NM_001083889.1) and IL-10 (for mouse, RefSeq: NM_010548.2; for monkey, RefSeq: XM_005540653.3).

Plasmids encoding an empty vector, or CTLA4-Ig, PD-L1 and IL-10 in the absence of FAP-specific scFv (CPIDCs), as well as constructs encoding GFP-specific scFv together with CTLA4-Ig, PD-L1 and IL-10 (GFP-CPIDCs), were generated as control vectors. Moreover, plasmids incorporating murine FAP-specific scFv, as well as single immunomodulatory component (FAP-C, FAP-P or FAP-I) or dual combinations of these factors (FAP-CP, FAP-CI and FAP-PI) were constructed. All plasmids were synthesized by Guangzhou IGE Biotechnology. The integrity of constructs was confirmed by restriction enzyme digestion and sequencing analyses.

Lentivirus production

Lentivirus was produced following our established protocol32. Plasmid DNA used for lentiviral packaging was purified using NucleoBond Xtra Maxi EF kit (Macherey-Nagel, 740420.50). Confluent HEK293FT packaging cells were seeded the day before at a 1:2 dilution into 15 cm dishes. Two packaging plasmids, psPAX2 (Addgene, 12260) and pMD2.G (Addgene, 12259), together with respective DC engineering plasmids or control plasmids, were diluted in Opti-MEM (Gibco, 31985070) containing 1 mg ml−1 PEI (Yeasen, 40816ES03) at a DNA:PEI ratio of 1:3-1:4. The plasmid mixtures were transfected into the cells. After a 10 h incubation, the medium was replaced with fresh DMEM complete medium. Then, 72 h after transfection, lentivirus was collected using PEG8000 (Sigma-Aldrich, 89510) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

Generation of BMDCs and lentivirus transduction

For the preparation of mouse of bone-marrow-derived DCs (BMDCs), bone marrow was collected under sterile conditions, filtered through a 70 μm cell strainer (Biosharp, BS-70-XBS) and treated with red blood cell lysis buffer (Solarbio, R1010) for 5 min to obtain a leukocyte single-cell suspension. The cells were then cultured in RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco, C11875500BT) containing 10% FBS, 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin, 20 ng ml−1 recombinant murine granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) (PeproTech, 315-03-50) and 10 ng ml−1 recombinant murine interleukin-4 (IL-4) (PeproTech, 214-14-50). Cultures were maintained at 37 °C with 5% CO2, with an equal volume of fresh medium supplemented with the same concentrations of GM-CSF and IL-4 added every other day. After 7 days of culture, cells were transduced with lentivirus at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 10 in the presence of 10 μg ml−1 protamine sulfate.

For the preparation of monkey BMDCs, bone marrow samples were collected by aspiration from the posterior superior iliac spine to prepare a single-cell suspension. The cells were then cultured in RPMI 1640 supplemented with 10% FBS, 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin, 100 ng ml−1 recombinant rhesus GM-CSF (MCE, HY-P7184) and macrophage colony-stimulating factor (M-CSF) (MCE, HY-P73831). On day 7, the culture medium was replaced with fresh RPMI 1640 supplemented with 10% FBS, 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin, 100 ng ml−1 recombinant rhesus IL-4 (MCE, HY-P71859) and GM-CSF. On day 14, the cells were transduced with lentivirus at a MOI of 10 in the presence of 8 μg ml−1 protamine sulfate. Both mouse and monkey DC cultures were tested for mycoplasma every 3 days.

The transduction efficiency of engineered DCs was assessed 48 h after lentiviral transduction by flow cytometry analysis of scFv expression, and by ELISA quantification of secreted CTLA4-Ig or IL-10. For scFv detection, either protein L-PE (58036, Cell Signaling Technology) or an antibody against G4S linker (Alexa Fluor 488 G4S Linker E7O2V, 50515, Cell Signaling Technology) was used. Protein L specifically binds to the κ light chain of immunoglobulins and therefore recognizes scFvs containing a κ variable domain, whereas the G4S linker antibody detects the flexible linker connecting the variable heavy (VH) and variable light (VL) domains, enabling confirmation of structural scFv expression.

Co-culture of iCDCs with T cells

T cells from the spleens of C57BL/6 mice were isolated using the mouse CD3ε MicroBead Kit (Miltenyi Biotec, 130-094-973) and cultured in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS, 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin, 100 U ml−1 recombinant murine IL-2 (PeproTech, 212-12-50), 50 μM β-mercaptoethanol (Sigma-Aldrich, M3148) and 10 mM HEPES (Gibco, 15630106). Cells were stimulated with mouse anti-CD3/CD28 Dynabeads (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 11452D) with a bead-to-cell ratio of 1:1 for 2 days. After activation, the beads were removed magnetically. Each well of a 24-well plate was seeded with 5 × 105 T cells alone or with 1 × 105 WT DCs, vecDCs or iCDCs, in complete RPMI 1640 medium for 2 days. For blocking experiments with monoclonal antibodies, anti-mouse IL-10 antibody (JES052A5, R&D Systems, MAB417), anti-mouse CTLA4 antibody (63828, R&D Systems, MAB434) and anti-mouse PD-L1 antibody (2341A, R&D Systems, MAB90783) were added at the beginning of the co-culture to a final concentration of 10 μg ml−1. The activation status of T cells in the co-culture was assessed by flow cytometry.

T cell proliferation assay

T cells were isolated from the spleens of C57BL/6 mice, stimulated by mouse anti-CD3/CD28 Dynabeads for 2 days and labelled with 5 μM CFSE (Invitrogen, C34554). Each well of a 24-well plate was seeded with 5 × 105 CFSE-labelled T cells alone or with 1 × 105 WT DCs, vecDCs or iCDCs in complete T cell medium. Monoclonal antibodies against mouse IL-10, CTLA4 or PD-L1 were added to the co-cultures to achieve a final concentration of 10 μg ml−1. After co-culture for 2 days, T cell proliferation was assessed by CFSE dilution using flow cytometry.

Co-culture of iCDCs with macrophages

Bone-marrow-derived macrophages were generated according to a protocol similar to that of BMDCs. In brief, bone marrow cells were maintained in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS, 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin and 20 ng ml−1 recombinant murine macrophage colony-stimulating factor (M-CSF) (PeproTech, 315-02-10) for 7 days, along with replacement of the medium every other day. Macrophages were pretreated with 100 ng ml−1 LPS (Sigma-Aldrich, L4391) and 20 ng ml−1 recombinant murine IFNγ (PeproTech, 315-05) for 2 days to generate M1-like macrophages, 2 × 105 of which were seeded into each well of a 24-well plate alone or with 2 × 105 WT DCs or iCDCs, in complete RPMI 1640 medium for 2 days. At the end of co-culture, macrophages were collected and their immune status was assessed by flow cytometry analysis.

Co-culture of iCDCs with B cells

B cells from the spleens of C57BL/6 mice were isolated using the mouse CD19 MicroBeads (Miltenyi Biotec, 130-121-301) and cultured in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin. B cells were stimulated with anti-mouse CD40 antibody (5 μg ml−1, Selleck, A2136), IL-2 (100 U ml−1), IL-4 (10 ng ml−1) and LPS (2 μg ml−1) for 2 days. Each well of a 24-well plate was seeded with 5 × 105 B cells alone or with 1 × 105 WT DCs or iCDCs, in complete RPMI 1640 medium for 2 days. After the co-culture, B cells (CD11c−CD19+) were examined by flow cytometry for IgG+ B cells.

Co-culture of iCDCs with bone marrow-derived neutrophils

Neutrophils from the bone marrow of C57BL/6 mice were isolated using the mouse Neutrophil Isolation Kit (Miltenyi Biotec, 130-097-658) and cultured in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin. Each well of a 24-well plate was seeded with 5 × 105 neutrophils alone or with 1 × 105 WT DCs or iCDCs in complete RPMI 1640 medium for 24 h. After the co-culture, neutrophils were examined by flow cytometry for the expression of CD14 and ICAM-1.

Co-culture of iCDCs with fibroblasts

Fibroblasts from the hearts of adult C57BL/6 mice were obtained as previously described33. In brief, after the removal of the heart base, the remaining cardiac tissue was finely minced and digested with 3 ml of enzyme solution, containing 2 mg ml−1 collagenase IV (Worthington, LS004188) and 1.2 U ml−1 dispase II (Sigma-Aldrich, D4693), at 37 °C in a shaking incubator. The suspension was gently triturated with a pipette every 15 min during the 45 min digestion to enhance tissue dissociation. The digestion was terminated by adding an equal volume of low-glucose DMEM (Gibco, 11885084) supplemented with 20% FBS. The remaining cell suspension was filtered through a 40-μm strainer, resuspended in low-glucose DMEM containing 10% FBS and seeded onto gelatin-coated culture dishes. After 90 min of incubation, non-adherent cells were removed by medium replacement, and adherent fibroblasts were cultured in low-glucose DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin for 2 days. Each well of a six-well plate was seeded with 2 × 105 fibroblasts treated with 10 ng ml−1 TGF-β (MCE, HY-P7117) alone or with 2 × 105 WT DCs or iCDCs in complete RPMI 1640 medium for an additional 2 days. After co-culture, DCs were removed by CD11C microbeads (Miltenyi Biotec, 130-125-835), and the activation status of fibroblasts was evaluated by qPCR with reverse transcription (RT–qPCR) and flow cytometry.

ELISA

Culture supernatants were collected from WT DCs, vecDCs or iCDCs. The concentrations of IL-10 and CTLA4-Ig secreted by mouse and monkey iCDCs were quantified using species-specific ELISA kits, including the Mouse IL-10 ELISA kit (ABclonal, RK04490), Mouse CTLA4 ELISA kit (SAB, EK19533), Monkey IL-10 ELISA kit (Abcam, ab119530) and Human CTLA4 ELISA kit (SAB, EK13503), according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

Cytokine levels in the serum were measured using ELISA kits according to the manufacturers’ instructions. In the mouse I/R model, peripheral blood was collected at day 7 after I/R injury to quantify the serum levels of IFNγ (Abcam, ab282874), IL-6 (ABclonal, RK04845), TNF (Thermo Fisher Scientific, BMS607-3), IL-17α (ABclonal, RK00039), IL-4 (ABclonal, RK00036) and IL-10 using the corresponding mouse ELISA kits. In the monkey MI model experiments, peripheral blood was obtained at the baseline, after MI surgery (day 2), and on days 4, 7 and 14 after MI. Serum concentrations of IL-6 (Abcam, ab242233), IL-10, IL-17α (Wuhan Fine Biotech, EMK0186) and IFNγ (Abcam, ab270895) were determined using monkey-specific ELISA kits.

Flow cytometry analysis

For identification of surface markers of in vitro cultured DCs, WT DCs, vecDCs and iCDCs were stimulated by LPS (200 ng ml−1) and TNF (20 ng ml−1) for 24 h, then cells were collected and stained for flow cytometry. For cardiac cell analysis, single-cell suspensions were prepared from mouse heart tissue for flow cytometry analysis33. In brief, mice were euthanized, perfused through the left ventricle with cold PBS and the hearts were excised. Atria and valves were trimmed away, and the ventricles were diced into 1 mm3 fragments. Minced tissue was digested in 2 ml of digestion buffer (2 mg ml−1 collagenase IV; Worthington Biochemical, LS004188) at 37 °C for 15 min, with gentle mechanical trituration using 1,000 µl micropipettes. After allowing the mixture to settle for 1 min, the supernatant was transferred into stopping buffer (DMEM supplemented with 20% FBS). The remaining tissue underwent two additional rounds of digestion. All of the supernatants were pooled, filtered through a 70-µm cell strainer and centrifuged at 500g for 5 min. Red blood cells were lysed using red blood cell lysis buffer. The resulting single-cell suspensions were subjected to staining for flow cytometry analysis.

To block Fc receptors, cells were first incubated with anti-CD16/32 antibody (BD Biosciences, 553142), followed by viability staining using Fixable Viability Stain 700 (BD Biosciences, 564997). Cells were then incubated at 4 °C for 30 min with fluorophore-conjugated anti-mouse antibodies for surface antigen detection. For intracellular IL17 detection, single-cell suspensions were stimulated for 6 h in complete T cell medium containing leukocyte-activation cocktail (BD Biosciences, 550583) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. For intracellular staining, cells were subsequently fixed and permeabilized using the Cytofix/Cytoperm Fixation/Permeabilization Kit (BD Biosciences, 554714) according to the manufacturer’s instructions, and subsequently incubated with intracellular antibodies diluted in Perm/Wash buffer at 4 °C for 30 min. After washing twice with DPBS containing 1% BSA, the samples were analysed on a BD LSR II flow cytometer, and data were analysed using FlowJo software. Detailed information on antibodies used for flow cytometry is provided in Supplementary Table 1, and the gating strategies for flow cytometry analysis and cell sorting are shown in Extended Data Fig. 14.

Western blot

Total protein was extracted from homogenizing tissues lysed in RIPA buffer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 89901) containing protease and phosphatase inhibitor cocktail (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 78443) for 30 min on ice. After centrifugation at 12,000g for 15 min at 4 °C, the supernatants were collected as total protein extracts. Protein samples were separated on 7.5% SDS–PAGE gels and transferred onto nitrocellulose membranes (Millipore, HATF00010). After blocking with 5% skimmed milk in TBST for 1 h at room temperature, the membranes were incubated with primary antibodies overnight at 4 °C, followed by incubation with secondary antibodies at room temperature for 1 h. Protein bands were detected using the Bio-Rad Image Lab Touch Software v.3.0 followed by visualization with Clarity Max Western ECL Substrate (Bio-Rad, 1705062). Band intensity quantification was performed using Image Lab software (Bio-Rad). Details of the antibodies used are provided in Supplementary Table 1.

RT–qPCR

Total RNA was extracted using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, 15596018) followed by chloroform extraction and reverse transcribed into cDNA using the RT reagent kit (Takara, RR047A). qPCR was performed using the FastStart Universal TB Green reagent (Takara, RR820A) on the ABI Prism 7000 system (Applied Biosystems). The primer sequences for RT–qPCR are listed in Supplementary Table 2.

Mouse I/R and MI models

I/R and MI injuries in mice were surgically induced as previously described34,35. In brief, mice were anaesthetized by intraperitoneal injection of sodium pentobarbital (1%, 60 mg per kg). After loss of pedal reflexes, tracheal intubation was performed. Mechanical ventilation was then initiated using a rodent ventilator (ALC-V8S, Shanghai Alcott Biotechnology). After opening the chest cavity at the third intercostal space, the left anterior descending (LAD) coronary artery was exposed and isolated. For the I/R model, the LAD artery was temporarily ligated approximately 2 mm from its origin with a 7-0 silk suture threaded through PE-10 tubing. Successful ischaemia was confirmed by visible myocardial blanching, akinesis of the downstream myocardium and ST-segment elevation on the electrocardiogram. After 45 min ischaemia, the ligature was carefully released to initiate reperfusion, which was confirmed by restoration of myocardial coloration and contractile motion, accompanied by the resolution of ST-segment. For MI induction, permanent LAD ligation was performed at the same location. Sham surgeries were performed in the same way but without ligation of the LAD coronary artery. Finally, the chest wall was closed by sequentially suturing the muscle and skin layers. On day 3 after the operation, mice meeting model-specific EF thresholds (I/R < 70%, MI < 55%) were identified by echocardiography and randomly assigned to experimental groups.

For tissue collection, defined regions of the left ventricle were isolated according to the experimental model36,37. In the I/R model, the ischaemic area at risk, defined as the myocardium previously subjected to transient coronary occlusion, was collected to perform bulk RNA-seq, scRNA-seq and flow cytometry analysis. In the MI model, the infarct zone and border zone were isolated for single-cell TCR sequencing and flow cytometry analysis. These defined regions were consistently collected across all groups to minimize sampling-related variation.

Mouse TAC model

After anaesthesia through intraperitoneal injection of sodium pentobarbital, a thoracotomy was performed at the second intercostal space to expose the aortic arch and left common carotid artery. After blunt dissection, a 6-0 silk suture was passed beneath the arch between the brachiocephalic and left carotid arteries. A 27-gauge needle was placed parallel to the aorta as a spacer during ligation to create standardized constriction, after which the needle was removed38. Mice were assigned to experimental groups based on two distinct protocols according to various study objectives: (1) mice were randomly allocated at 4 weeks after TAC; (2) mice were randomly allocated upon detection of EF < 40%. Sham surgeries were performed in the same way but without constriction of the aorta.

Engineered DC injection

For I/R and MI mice, a single dose of 1.5 × 106 cells suspended in sterile DPBS (150 μl) was delivered through tail vein injection at 3 days after surgery. For dose–response analysis, low-dose (1.5 × 106 cells in 150 μl DPBS) or high-dose (3 × 106 cells in 150 μl DPBS) treatments were administered at 3 days after surgery. To define the optimal timing of iCDC administration, 1.5 × 106 cells in 150 μl DPBS were administered on day 1, 3 or 7 after I/R. Control mice received 150 μl DPBS according to the same procedure. For TAC mice, two sequential cell injections (1.5 × 106 cells in 150 μl sterile DPBS each) were administered through the tail vein: the first dose was administered immediately after group assignment (either at 4 weeks after TAC or after EF ≦ 40% detection), followed by a second dose 2 weeks later.

Biodistribution analysis of engineered DCs

The biodistribution of engineered DCs was assessed using the lipophilic membrane dye DiR according to the manufacturer’s instructions. In brief, vecDCs, CPIDC, GFP-CPIDC or iCDCs were resuspended at a density of 1 × 106 cells per ml in DPBS containing 5 μM DiR iodide (Yeasen, 40757ES25) and incubated at 37 °C for 20 min protected from light. After labelling, cells were washed three times with an equal volume of RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS, resuspended in sterile DPBS and immediately administered through the tail vein (3 × 106 cells per mouse in 150 μl DPBS). At 1, 3, 7 and 14 days after injection, the mice were euthanized and the hearts and other major organs (lung, liver, spleen, kidney) were collected for ex vivo fluorescence imaging using the AniView100 Multimodal In Vivo Imaging System (Bio-View). All imaging parameters (excitation/emission = 748/820 nm) were maintained uniformly across all groups.

Echocardiography assessment of mice

Cardiac function was evaluated using the Vevo F2 ultrasound imaging system (FUJIFILM VisualSonics) equipped with an UHF57x transducer (25–57 MHz). Mice were anaesthetized with isoflurane and positioned on a heating pad to maintain body temperature. Imaging was commenced after the animal regained consciousness, as indicated by the restoration of physiological heart rates. Parasternal long-axis views and short-axis views were acquired in both B-mode and M-mode imaging for structural and functional analysis. The following parameters were collected: heart rate (HR), left ventricular internal dimension at end-diastole (LVIDd), left ventricular internal dimension at end-systole (LVIDs), ejection fraction (EF) and fractional shortening (FS). At least three consecutive cardiac cycles were analysed using Vevo LAB software with blinded operator assessment.

For I/R model mice, echocardiography was performed at baseline (pre-surgery), 3 days, 4 weeks, 8 weeks and 12 weeks after surgery. For the MI model mice, echocardiography assessments were conducted at the baseline and 3 and 28 days after surgery. For TAC model mice, two echocardiography protocols were implemented: (1) for mice randomized at 4 weeks after TAC, examinations were performed at the baseline, 4 weeks, 6 weeks and 8 weeks after surgery; (2) for mice randomized after confirmed EF < 40%, echocardiography assessments were performed at the baseline (pre-surgery), pre-treatment (at randomization), 2 weeks and 4 weeks after treatment initiation.

Electrophysiology and arrhythmia assessment of mice

ECG recordings were collected using a rechargeable wireless electrocardiogram acquisition backpack (Ensen-1E, Enshi Scientific Research) connected to a PowerLab 16/35 (PL3516, AD INSTRUMENTS) recording system. Mice were anaesthetized with isoflurane, and positive electrodes for simulated limb lead II recordings were implanted subcutaneously at the right axillary region, then negative electrodes inserted into the intersection of the left anterior superior iliac spine and the lateral edge of the rectus abdominis. Electrode shape and placement were repeatedly tested and gradually adjusted until clear ECG signals with stable baseline were obtained. After successful implantation, 1.5 × 106 cells suspended in 150 μl DPBS were injected, while control mice received an equal volume of DPBS. Mice were housed individually for postoperative recovery and monitored continuously for 7 days. The episodes and duration of non-sustained ventricular tachycardia and sustained ventricular tachycardia, as well as the occurrence of premature ventricular contractions, were quantified. ECG data were analysed using LabChart8 with blinded assessment by cardiology specialists.

T cell depletion experiment

Systemic TCRaβ+ T cell depletion was achieved by intraperitoneal administration of anti-mouse TCRβ monoclonal antibody (H57-597, Bio X Cell, BE0102) at a dose of 10 mg per kg. Treatment was commenced 1 day before surgery and subsequently repeated every 4 days. The depletion of TCRaβ+ T cells was confirmed by flow cytometry.

NHP MI model and iCDC treatment

The induction of MI in monkeys was performed according to established surgical protocols39. Anaesthesia was first induced through intramuscular injection of ketamine (5 mg per kg) and midazolam (0.2 mg per kg). Once anaesthetized, the animals were intubated and mechanically ventilated with room air using a ventilator (Matrx VIP 3000, Midmark) throughout the surgical procedure. A left thoracotomy was then carried out to provide access to the heart, followed by a pericardiotomy to expose the myocardium. To induce MI, the LAD coronary artery was permanently ligated distal to its first branch using a 5-0 silk suture (Ethicon, W8556). The success of the procedure was confirmed by a visible darkening of the myocardial tissue distal to the ligation, indicative of ischaemia, as well as ST-segment elevation observed on electrocardiography. After completion of the ligation, the chest cavity was carefully closed in layers, and animals were monitored during recovery. Postoperative monkeys were provided with morphine for analgesia and penicillin to prevent infection.

On postoperative day 3, monkeys in the treatment groups received intravenous cell injections of 3.5–7 × 106 iCDCs in 50 ml saline through the saphenous vein, while control monkeys received 50 ml saline.

Echocardiography assessment of NHPs

Echocardiography assessments were conducted at the baseline (pre-surgery) and 2 days, 1 month, 2 months and 3 months after MI. Transthoracic echocardiography was performed on monkeys by an experienced echocardiologist in a blinded manner. The animals were sedated through intramuscular injections of Zoteil (4 mg per kg), and the procedure was conducted using an Ultrasound system (iE Elite, Philips Healthcare) equipped with an X7-2 transducer (2–7 MHz). 2D images were captured in both the parasternal long-axis and short-axis views. M-mode echocardiography was performed at the level of the mitral valve leaflet tips in the parasternal long-axis view. EF was calculated using the biplane Simpson method from 2D images obtained in the apical four-chamber and two-chamber views, following the guidelines of the American Society of Echocardiography.

MRI analysis of monkeys

Monkeys were anaesthetized with a combination of Zoteil (4 mg per kg) and xylazine (0.2 mg per kg), then placed in dorsal recumbency. All MR data were acquired on a 3.0 T MRI system (uMR 880, United Imaging Healthcare). Cardiac volumes and function were assessed using images from a balanced steady-state free precession sequence with retrospective ECG gating without breathing control. A stack of short-axis planes (6 mm thickness) and three long-axis planes (2-, 3- and 4-chamber) were obtained. The acquisition parameters were as follows: TR = 3.39 ms, TE = 1.53 ms, flip angle = 55°, field of view = 200 × 180 mm2, voxel size = 1.23 × 1.04 mm2. A phase-sensitive inversion recovery (PSIR) gradient echo sequence was performed 10 min after the injection of 0.2 mmol kg−1 gadodiamide after TI scout imaging to determine the optimal inversion time. PSIR images included two-, three- and four-chamber views as well as short-axis views. The acquisition parameters were as follows: TR = 5.59 ms, TE = 2.37 ms, flip angle = 15°, field of view = 200 × 200 mm2, voxel size = 0.89 × 0.89 mm2. Cardiac MRI was performed pre-MI and at 2 days, 1 month and 3 months after MI. All CMR images were analysed by blinded investigators. Cine and PSIR images were analysed offline using Qmass (v.8.1, Medis). The left ventricular ejection fraction was analysed based on a consecutive short axis stack of cine images. The following parameters were obtained in the meantime: left ventricular end-systolic volume (LVESV), left ventricular end-diastolic volume (LVEDV). LGE extent was determined as the proportion of total myocardium (infarct mass divided by total left ventricle mass).

Cranial MRI was performed at 3 months after MI. The examination protocol of cranial MRI included a T2-weighted sequence for all monkeys. Axial T2-weighted images were acquired parallel to the anterior commissure–posterior commissure (AC–PC) line. The acquisition parameters were as follows: TR = 3,647.0 ms, TE = 104.0 ms, flip angle = 167°, field of view = 576 × 576 mm2, voxel size = 0.35 × 0.35 × 4.40 mm3.

Electrophysiology and arrhythmia assessment in NHPs

Continuous cardiac electrophysiological monitoring was performed from day 3 after surgery (before cell injection) until day 28 after injection using an attachable, ultra-long-term ambulatory ECG monitoring system (NS-SP-A-01). The system comprises a wearable ECG recorder, a dedicated data reading dock and an ultra-long-term dynamic ECG analysis system. Animals were sedated by intramuscular injection of Zoteil (2 mg per kg) to facilitate the placement of the monitoring patch onto the anterior chest. After patch placement, each animal was fitted with a protective vest to prevent dislodgement of the device. ECG signals were acquired through the monitoring patch, with single-channel ECG data stored in real time in the embedded EMMC storage. Throughout the monitoring period, the stored raw ECG data were transferred daily to a computer workstation through a dedicated reading dock. The complete continuous ECG signals were then analysed and visualized using the ultra-long-term dynamic ECG analysis system (TaiDoc, Dongguan; v.Z1). The retrieved data were analysed in a blinded manner by cardiac electrophysiology specialists. The episodes and duration of non-sustained ventricular tachycardia and sustained ventricular tachycardia, as well as the occurrence of premature ventricular contractions, were quantified.

Blood biochemistry and haematological analysis

In the mouse model, peripheral blood samples were collected at 28 days after I/R and MI, and at 56 days after TAC. Serum biochemical markers, including ALT, AST, BUN and Cr, were assessed at the Laboratory Animal Research Center of Zhejiang Chinese Medical University.

In the monkey MI model, peripheral blood was collected at the baseline and on days 2, 4, 7, 14, 28, 60 and 90 after MI. In brief, the animals were anaesthetized with Zoletil (3 mg per kg), and blood samples obtained through femoral venipuncture. Approximately 1.5 ml of blood was collected in EDTA-K2 tubes for whole-blood analysis, and 3 ml of blood was collected in serum separation tubes for serum extraction. Complete blood counts and serum biochemical markers, including white blood cell count, platelet count, haemoglobin concentration, albumin/globulin ratio, ALT, AST and Cr, were measured at the Clinical Laboratory of the Second Affiliated Hospital, Zhejiang University.

Sirius Red staining and fibrosis calculation

To evaluate cardiac fibrosis, mouse hearts were embedded in paraffin, whereas monkey hearts were embedded in optimal cutting temperature (OCT) compound (Sakura Finetek, 4583) and cryosectioned. Serial sections (4 µm for mouse hearts; 7 µm for monkey hearts) were obtained continuously from the apex to the base of each heart. The sections were stained with Sirius Red (Sbjbio, BP-DL029) for 60 min at room temperature, followed by washing in 95% ethanol and sequential dehydration through graded ethanol series. The fibrotic area was quantified using ImageJ by calculating the mean percentage of Sirius-Red-positive area (stained red) relative to the total myocardial tissue area per section.

H&E staining

For safety evaluation, key organs from both mice (liver, kidney and lung) and monkeys (liver, lung, colon, intestine, kidney, spleen, brain, skin and thyroid) were embedded in paraffin and sectioned. Sections were stained with H&E according to the following protocol: after dewaxing (xylene I/II, 10 min each) and rehydration (graded ethanol series), the sections were stained with Harris haematoxylin (5 min), differentiated (1% acid alcohol, 30 s), blued (15 min water rinse), counterstained with eosin Y (0.5%, 2 min), dehydrated (reverse ethanol series), cleared (xylene) and mounted. The stained sections were evaluated by an experienced pathologist.

Immunofluorescence staining and analysis

Paraffin-embedded mouse heart sections were subjected to deparaffinization with xylene, rehydration through a graded ethanol series and antigen retrieval. Frozen monkey heart sections were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde (Beyotime, P0099) for 15 min at room temperature. All of the sections were then uniformly processed with 0.2% Triton X-100 (Yeasen, 20107ES20) for permeabilization (10 min), followed by blocking with 5% BSA in DPBS (60 min). The sections were incubated with primary antibodies (Supplementary Table 1) overnight at 4 °C, washed three times with DPBS and subsequently incubated with fluorophore-conjugated secondary antibodies for 1 h at room temperature. Finally, nuclei were counterstained with DAPI (Sigma-Aldrich, F6057) for 5 min.

Images were acquired using the Leica DM6 B microscope and Zeiss LSM 980 laser-scanning microscope. Quantitative analyses were performed as follows: (1) cardiomyocyte area within the MI segment was quantified as the percentage of cTnI+ area relative to the sectional area; (2) vascular densities were determined by the number of CD31+ vessels or SMA+ vessels. For each heart, at least five high-power fields were randomly captured and analysed by investigators blinded to experimental groups.

Bulk RNA-seq

Total RNA was extracted from DCs cultured for 3 days after viral transduction or flow cytometry-sorted CD3+ T cells or heart tissues using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, 15596018CN). RNA libraries were generated using the TruSeq Stranded Total RNA Sample Preparation kit (Illumina) and sequenced on the NovaSeq 6000 platform. RNA-seq was conducted by Novogene.

For heart tissue samples, after quality assessment of raw sequencing data with FastQC (v.0.12.1) and adapter trimming with BBMap (v.39.26), reads were aligned to the mouse reference genome using STAR (v.2.7.11b), followed by transcript-level quantification with Salmon (v.1.10.1) based on the GRCm39 mouse reference genome (GENCODE release M34). Transcript-level estimates were summarized to gene-level count matrices using tximport (v.1.32.0) for downstream differential expression analysis.

For in vitro samples, raw FASTQ data were processed using another standardized pipeline, including quality control with fastp (v.0.23.1), alignment with HISAT2 (v.2.0.5), reference-guided transcript assembly with StringTie (v.1.3.3b) and gene-level read counting with featureCounts (v.1.5.0-p3) to generate gene-level expression matrices for downstream differential expression analysis.

Differential expression analysis was performed with DESeq2 (v.1.40.1). Before analysis, genes with extremely low expression were filtered out. Protein-coding genes exhibiting |log2[FC]| > 1 with P < 0.01 were defined as DEGs for heart tissue samples, whereas in vitro cell DEGs were defined as |log2[FC]| > 1 and FDR < 0.05. To facilitate comparability across datasets, downstream GO/KEGG enrichment and visualization were conducted using an identical workflow. The top 3,000 most significant DEGs, ranked by adjusted P value, were selected for GO and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analyses using the clusterProfiler package (v.4.14.6).

Analysis of spatial transcriptome data

For the analysis of the Stereo-seq spatial transcriptome data, we used datasets from the sham operation group and various time points (1, 3, 7, 14 and 28 days) after MI, as previously reported in our study40. Detailed procedures for Stereo-seq chip preparation, tissue sectioning, fixation, staining, imaging, library preparation, sequencing and data preprocessing can be found in our previous publication. To overcome the low RNA capture efficiency of individual DNB spots at a resolution of 500 nm, the raw spatial expression matrix was aggregated into larger pseudo-spots, each corresponding to a 25 µm square (50 × 50 window size, referred to as bin50). For enhanced visualization of Fap expression patterns across the spatial maps, data imputation was performed using R MAGIC package (v.2.0.3) with the default parameters.

scRNA-seq analysis

Viable cardiac non-myocytes from I/R mouse hearts were isolated for scRNA-seq using a Langendorff-free method41. In brief, anaesthetized adult mice underwent thoracotomy, and hearts were excised after right ventricular injection of 7 ml EDTA buffer with aortic clamping. The clamped heart was submerged in EDTA buffer, followed by 10 ml EDTA injection into the left ventricle apex and 3 ml perfusion buffer. The heart was then transferred to collagenase buffer (1.33 mg ml−1 type II + 1.66 mg ml−1 type IV) and perfused through the left ventricle apex with 30–40 ml until digested. After dissection, tissue was gently pipetted into ~1 mm3 fragments, filtered through a 100 µm strainer and subjected to 20-min gravity settling for myocyte sedimentation. The non-myocyte-containing supernatant was collected and centrifuged for downstream processing. Single-cell suspensions were then treated with red blood cell lysis buffer to remove erythrocytes, and dead cells were eliminated using the Dead Cell Removal Kit (Miltenyi Biotec, 130-090-101). The resulting suspensions were either directly processed for scRNA-seq or enriched for CD45+ immune cells by flow cytometry before library preparation. Single-cell suspensions were captured and processed using the Chromium Single Cell 3’ Reagent Kit v3 (10x Genomics) according to the Chromium Single Cell 3’ v3.1 protocol. Sequencing was performed on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 system with 150 bp paired-end reads, targeting a sequencing depth of 50,000 reads per cell.

Library preparation and scRNA-seq were performed by LC-Bio Technology. Raw sequencing reads were aligned to the mouse reference genome (mm10) using the 10x Genomics Cell Ranger pipeline (v.7.2.0) to obtain gene expression count matrices. Downstream processing was performed with Seurat (v.5,3.0) and other R packages. First, cells underwent rigorous quality control: retained cells met these criteria—detected genes > 300 but below the top 1% quantile, UMIs > 500 but below the top 2% quantile, mitochondrial content ≤ 5% and haemoglobin expression ≤ 5%. Potential doublets were excluded through parametric bootstrap modelling with DoubletFinder (v.2.0.4).

Before downstream analysis, mitochondrial genes and ribosomal protein genes were removed from the Seurat object. Technical artifacts were mitigated by regressing out cell-cycle effects during SCTransform normalization. Principal component analysis was then performed using the top 50 principal components to capture maximal biological variation.

Sample batch effects were corrected by Harmony (v.1.2.3) integration. Unsupervised clustering proceeded through shared nearest-neighbour graph construction (FindNeighbors), community detection (FindClusters) and UMAP visualization (RunUMAP). Parameters were optimized as follows: reduction dimensions (dims = 15–40) determined by Elbow Plot inflection points, and resolution (0.8–1.6) calibrated with clustree to balance cluster granularity.

For subclustering of specific cell types, target cells were subsetted. Highly variable genes were recomputed, followed by iterative clustering with concurrent removal of contaminating cells. Cell-type annotation used conserved marker genes cross-referenced against CellMarker, PanglaoDB and published literature. Ccr2+/− MHC-IIhigh macrophage subsets were manually isolated based on Ccr2 expression in MHC-IIhigh macrophages.

DEGs were identified using FindAllMarkers/FindMarkers (Wilcoxon rank-sum test; thresholds: |log2[FC]| > 0.25, min.pct ≥ 0.25). Functional enrichment analysis for GO and KEGG terms was performed with clusterProfiler (enrichGO/enrichKEGG), applying Benjamini–Hochberg correction; redundant GO terms were pruned using simplify. ECM dynamics were quantified by calculating ECM regulator scores (AddModuleScore), using curated gene sets from MatrisomeDB v.2.0 with background expression normalization. The R package SCP (v.0.5.6) together with ggplot2 (v.3.5.2) were used for single-cell data visualization and feature-level statistical summaries across treatments and cell subtypes (for example, FeatureStatPlot).

Single-cell TCR sequencing

The method for preparing single-cell suspensions was consistent with that described in the ‘Flow cytometry analysis’ section. Single-cell suspensions prepared from heart tissues or peripheral blood of MI mice were enriched for CD45+CD3+CD4+ immune cells by fluorescence-activated cell sorting. Cells were then resuspended at a concentration of approximately 700–1,200 cells per µl. scRNA-seq libraries were prepared using the Chromium Next GEM Single Cell 5′ Kit v2 (10x Genomics, PN-1000263) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. In brief, cell suspensions were loaded onto the Chromium chip along with barcoded gel beads and partitioning oil to generate gel beads-in-emulsions (GEMs). After GEM formation, reverse transcription and cDNA pre-amplification were performed, and the resulting full-length cDNA was subsequently processed into transcriptome libraries using the Chromium Single Cell 5′ Library Construction Kit (10x Genomics, PN-1000190). In parallel, TCR libraries were constructed using the Chromium Single Cell V(D)J Enrichment Kit (10x Genomics, PN-1000071) and the Chromium Single Cell V(D)J Amplification Kit (10X Genomics, PN-1000254). Libraries were constructed and sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq platform using a paired-end 150 bp read length at OE Biotech.

Single-cell transcriptome and immunome sequencing data were processed using the Cell Ranger (v.9.0.0) cellranger multi pipeline with species-specific reference genomes (GRCm39-2024-A, Genecode). This integrated workflow simultaneously aligned gene expression libraries, assembled V(D)J sequences, and processed feature barcodes, using cell calls from transcriptome data to constrain V(D)J cell identification and prevent over-calling. Unique molecular identifiers were counted during alignment, with paired αβ chains assembled into clonotypes.

TCR repertoire integration and clonotype analysis were performed. Filtered contig annotation files from four biological contexts (MI_Blood, MI_heart, iCDC_Blood, iCDC_heart) were integrated into Seurat (v.5.3.0) objects using scRepertoire’s combineTCR function (v.2.5.0), retaining all productive TCR sequences without multi-chain filtering. Clonotype metadata were mapped to preclustered single-cell transcriptomes through combineExpression (amino-acid-level resolution; cloneCall = “aa”). Cells were stratified by clone expansion status: non-expanded (single-cell) versus expanded (multi-cell, subclassified as ≥2 cells).

Statistics and reproducibility

All in vitro cell culture experiments were independently repeated at least twice with similar results. Animal experiments were performed once for each condition unless indicated otherwise. Statistical analyses and graph generation were performed using GraphPad Prism (v.9 and v.10). The exact sample size (n) is indicated in each figure legend. Unless specified otherwise, comparisons between two groups were performed using unpaired two-tailed Student’s t-tests. Comparisons involving more than two groups were conducted using one-way ANOVA for single-factor designs or two-way ANOVA for two-factor designs, followed by Tukey’s multiple-comparisons test. Survival differences were analysed using Kaplan–-Meier curves and assessed using the log-rank (Mantel-Cox) test. For Fig. 2, all statistical tests were two-sided; two-way ANOVA with Tukey’s multiple-comparisons test was used for Fig. 2d,k, and one-way ANOVA with Tukey’s multiple-comparisons test was used for Fig. 2e–i,l,m. Data are presented as mean ± s.e.m. or mean ± s.d., as indicated in the figure legends. For box plots, the centre line denotes the median; the box limits represent the interquartile range (25th–75th percentiles); and the whiskers extend to the most extreme values within 1.5× the interquartile range. Exact P values are reported in the figures when P > 0.0001.
